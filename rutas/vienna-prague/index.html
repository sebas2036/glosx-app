Vienna to Prague by Train: Two Imperial Capitals

By WoW Train · Updated June 2026 · 4 min read

Two of Central Europe's most beautiful cities, joined by one easy ride. The direct train leaves Vienna, rolls north across the rolling farmland of Moravia and the Czech countryside, and delivers you straight into the heart of Prague — comfortable, frequent and far more scenic than the motorway.

The journey, stop by stop

You depart from Wien Hauptbahnhof, Vienna's sleek main station. The train heads north out of Austria and crosses into the Czech Republic, tracing the gentle hills and fields of Moravia — wine country, then the industrial-historic city of Brno.

From there it's a straight run across Bohemia toward the capital, the landscape softening into woods and river valleys as you approach the city.

Arrival is at Praha hlavní nádraží (Prague Main Station), an Art Nouveau landmark a short walk from the Old Town, Wenceslas Square and the Charles Bridge.

How long does it take?

The fastest direct trains cover the route in around 4 hours, and they run several times a day. Exact times shift by service and season, so check the live schedule for your date before you lock in plans.

Who runs the route?

The corridor is served by comfortable international trains — Austrian Railjet services and EuroCity trains operated together by the Austrian and Czech railways (ÖBB and ČD). Comparing the day's departures in one search is the easiest way to find the best time and fare.

How to get the best fare

  • Book early. The cheapest saver fares sell out first — booking ahead can be dramatically cheaper than buying on the day.
  • Travel off-peak. Mid-morning and mid-week departures tend to be quieter and cheaper.
  • Consider first class — on this route the upgrade is often modest and very comfortable for four hours.
  • Compare in one place to see every direct departure at a glance.

Why take the train instead of flying?

There's barely any point flying between Vienna and Prague — once you add airport transfers and waiting, the train is just as fast and drops you in the city centre. You also get a proper view of Central Europe rolling by, a power socket and the freedom to walk around. It's the way locals do it.
