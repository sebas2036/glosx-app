Paris to Amsterdam by Train: The Fast Direct

By WoW Train · Updated June 2026 · 4 min read

From the cafés of Paris to the canals of Amsterdam in a single high-speed hop. The direct train sweeps north out of France, calls in Brussels and the Low Countries, and glides into central Amsterdam in not much more than three hours — easily beating the plane once you count everything around a flight.

The journey, stop by stop

You leave from Paris Gare du Nord, Europe's busiest railway station. The train accelerates north across the plains of northern France toward Belgium, calling at Brussels — the de facto capital of the EU.

From there it crosses the Low Countries, stopping at Antwerp (with one of the most beautiful station halls in the world) and Rotterdam, before the flat, water-laced Dutch landscape signals you're nearly there.

Arrival is at Amsterdam Centraal, the grand red-brick station right on the edge of the canal ring — step out and you're already in the city.

How long does it take?

The fastest direct services cover the route in around 3 hours 20 minutes, with several departures a day. Exact times vary by service and season, so check the live schedule for your date before booking around it.

Who runs the route?

The corridor is run by Eurostar (the high-speed brand that absorbed the former Thalys), with other high-speed options also serving the line. Comparing the day's departures in one search is the easiest way to find the best time and fare.

How to get the best fare

  • Book early. High-speed fares on this route are dynamic — the earliest tickets are by far the cheapest.
  • Avoid Friday and Sunday evenings if you can; those peak departures cost the most.
  • Reservations are compulsory on these trains, so your seat is guaranteed — just book ahead.
  • Compare in one place to catch a cheaper departure on the same day.

Why take the train instead of flying?

Paris to Amsterdam is the textbook case for the train. The flight itself is short, but add the trek to the airport, security, boarding and the ride into town and the door-to-door train is genuinely faster — with city-centre stations at both ends, no baggage fees and a far smaller footprint.
